Archaeological Evidence for the Bible

Biblical archaeology provides powerful confirmation of the historical accuracy of Scripture. These ancient artifacts and inscriptions demonstrate that the Bible records real events, real people, and real places‚Äîvalidating the reliability of God's Word.

Below are key archaeological discoveries that corroborate the biblical narrative, from the Babylonian captivity to the Exodus from Egypt.

üè∫ The Cyrus Cylinder (c. 539-530 BC)

One of the most significant archaeological finds confirming biblical history, the Cyrus Cylinder records King Cyrus's decree allowing captive peoples to return to their homelands and rebuild their temples. This ancient clay cylinder, housed in the British Museum, provides extrabiblical evidence for the biblical account of the Jewish return from Babylonian exile (2 Chronicles 36:22-23, Ezra 1:2-4).

Remarkably, the prophet Isaiah prophesied about Cyrus by name 180 years before he was born, foretelling that this Persian king would allow Jerusalem to be rebuilt (Isaiah 44:28, 45:1-4).

Egyptian archaeological evidence provides fascinating insights into the possible identity of the Pharaoh of the Exodus. Thutmose I's reign (c. 1504-1492 BC) aligns remarkably with biblical details: his body was found in an unexpected coffin (possibly drowned), his firstborn sons died before reaching maturity (matching the tenth plague), and his daughter Hatshepsut may have been the princess who rescued Moses.

This archaeological study examines the evidence for dating the Exodus and identifying the Pharaoh who refused to let God's people go.
